  On Systems Engineering

    Good evening Tim and Aaron! I was on hiatus due to school and spent copious amounts of time wondering how Auburn STUNNED Oregon with twenty seconds left in the fourth quarter. This post will cover the pros and cons of Systems Engineering (SE) and how to effectively use the development process. A colleague once said SE is like salt; a little bit is great, but too much can be problematic. This is admittedly a bit paraphrased but holds true across companies and industry sectors. While useful in determining problems and brainstorming solutions, SE has limitations beyond early phases. Having spent a few years in the SE world, I will communicate its overall effectiveness.

  The Big Picture

    As Wikipedia's definition entails, SE is "an interdisciplinary field of engineering and engineering management that focuses on how to design and manage complex systems over their life cycles." SE focuses on the overarching design of products and engineering solutions. At its core, SE is management encapsulated by various engineering professions; which allows great visibility into a system's big picture. The most common SE approach is the System Development Life Cycle (SDLC), which frequently appears in various Information Technology (IT) certifications. A simplified life-cycle approach is below. The "V-model" from The International Council on Systems Engineering (INCOSE) is much more detailed.


This is ultimately the process of designing a product. A smart person has an idea, writes it down, but needs guidance on how to proceed. "What is the next step?" Referring to a concrete model simplifies the development process and causes less frustration in the beginning. In software engineering, a developer does not code their entire project all at once. That is usually too difficult for a significant project. Instead software engineers incrementally write and test the project to ensure its building blocks work. The development process, much like the system life cycle process, is a methodical journey with validation ensured.

  Breaking Down Problems

    As stated above, SE deals with problems of scale requiring many calculated steps. discipline primarily focuses on overarching design of products.

  Too High Level?

    Modularity in system design is great but is it perhaps too high in the sky for engineers of other disciplines? There is a reason SE contains "management" so prominently in its definition; because it IS a management-based engineering discipline. They LOVE their acronyms.

  Key Development Position

    With everything SE offers, why is there a vast age gap in the industry?

  More Meetings

    I have witnessed various #InfoSec personalities on Twitter state how much they loathe meetings; sometimes going into great detail. They will use Slack, email, and phone to ensure pointless meetings are non-existent. To them, meetings on their calendars are present for a legitimate reason; however, this is not necessarily the case for SE.

Depending on the industry, the focus and deliverables focus on meetings and documents than on producing the system itself. As previously mentioned, strong documentation skills can separate an engineer from the pack.

  "Butts in Seats"

    Although SE is a valid engineering field, certain industries are inundated with systems engineers to an unnecessary degree. This plethora causes a depression in the worth of their work. There are only so many engineers that can be thrown at a requirements document before waste occurs. It never occurs to managers or even system owners how much money, time, and effort is wasted at various levels.

This depression causes tons of downtime and ultimately boredom for more ambitious employees. Very prone to body shop type of work.
