  Education and Credentialing

        September 3, 2020

   A common thread that persists in #infosec Twitter is the value of certifications.  It seems most of the community reached a consensus that certifications are not the most important indicator of future job performance.  This comes as no surprise to those regularly engaged with the community.  This post is not meant to debate the point.  Instead, this post looks into why the debate arose in the first place and what the education landscape looks like in the U.S.  This post will be slightly philosophical as some issues stem from education as a larger entity; not exclusive to information security.
    Before rolling into everything, I will begin by stating my general opinion on certifications.  Not all certifications are equal.  Some are very worthwhile and establish baseline knowledge and can help "get a foot in the door" when looking for first opportunities.  Some are basically timesinks that provide little return on investment.  Many more certifications lie at various points between the two; like a spectrum of usefulness.  Whichever certifications you choose, it is best to go with ones that support whichever infosec jobs you want.


  Information Monopoly

   Universities have existed for a few thousand years with the first institutions of higher learning established around 2200 BCE.  Due to predominant technology, information existed at these institutions in a centralized manner.  And for at least half of that time, information was stored, transmitted, and shared orally then by print.  Johannes Gutenberg's printing press enabled easier transmission of information across Europe and eventually the New World.  Lately electronic media has given rise to mass communications enabling additional information transmission.  A new distributed manner of education has emerged in the form of online degrees and self-study classes.

   Decentralization of learning has disrupted traditional education methods.  For the vast majority of universities' existance, only elites or religious scholars had privilege to attend and access information.  This has created an economic impact as well since Millennials are the most educated but indebted generation in history.  Avocado toast had nothing to do with that.  The long term effects are not yet known but will likely enable further distributed learning environments.
   With mass information transmission in place, a new question arises: what does a formal university education provide?  Education has become saturated over the past few decades with parents pushing a college-no-matter-what ultimatum on high school graduates.  


 Education vs. Credentialing?

   The purpose of higher education is to transmit information in such a way that enables innovation.  


  Who Should Receive Education?

    Modularity in system design is great but is it perhaps too high in the sky for engineers of other disciplines?  
    Although SE is considered high-level, systems engineers are sometimes required to dig into the true meaning of requirements.  Sometimes this is a valid issue as catching mistakes early can save the program money long term.  What actually happens is tons of time is wasted analyzing "shall" statements in attempts to feign importance to the customer.  SE tends to breed an environment that rewards overcomplicating tasks to give an appearance that more work is being done than necessary.  This of course leads into why DODAF contains such an enourmous amount of charts and models; most of which are unnecessary.  When is an OV-6 diagram ever necessary?  Instead, SE should remain as simple as possible to allow effective communication of project goals.  Thankfully this is well done in other industry sectors.


  Not a Key Development Position (KDP)

    With everything SE offers, why is there a vast age gap in the industry?  The median age was upper 40s in most meetings I attended.  In reality, SE is suitable for engineers who have spent time developing products earlier in their careers.  A cybersecurity engineer would make a great systems engineer but the corollary is not necessarily true; which is funny because I made that exact jump from SE to cybersecurity.  However, I relied on my masters program when interviewing rather than my SE work.  A newly graduated engineer should not be going into SE if a business major could execute the same non-technical work such as action item tracking.  From a student's perspective, why attempt the rigor of engineering school if a business major can complete the same tasks?
    In the same vein as KDPs, continuing education recommendations are also disconcerning.  Because SE is managerial in nature, higher-ups often recommend getting a Masters of Business Administration (MBA) to "check the box" of simply holding a masters degree.  Unless the degree is from a top 10 business school, an MBA is basically worthless; but that topic is an entire post of its own.  If you are an engineer who enjoys learning and discovering how systems, computers, applied mathematics, or general natural environments work, this attitude is particularly alarming.  This is not the approach to continuing education that management should emphasize.  PhD holders are unicorns in SE environments.  Although they preach the values of post-graduate studies, management is extremely reluctant to pay for continuing education if they cannot use your future credentials to bid on work.  Peers have told me they had to jump through hoops for an employer to reimburse PhD courses.  "Why get a PhD?"  Publication and mastery of your field are apparently not enough.  For this reason alone, full-time SE sectors perpetuate anti-intellectual behavior in an ironic attempt to support masters degrees and continuing education.
    Even though SE bills itself as interdisciplinary, the work itself is one-dimensional.  A systems engineer does not directly develop the system solutions nor do they perform the actual integration of said solutions.  This means dedicated SE is a dead-end job in which lateral movement to other engineering fields are greatly hindered.  If the company structure is top-down with many levels of management, the effect is worsened and if the systems engineer happens to be in middle-management, they are essentially shoehorned to be a systems engineer for life. At that point, you better love your job because you are stuck!  In contrast, a software engineer learns about development, OSs, programming languages, data structures, and various technical aspects that plug into other engineering fields.  Unless career trajectory involves management, SE practitioners often lack the freedom of maneuver of other disciplines.  I recommend limiting dedicated SE jobs to two years if other engineering fields sound appealing and avoid entry/junior SE positions at all costs.


  More Meetings

    I have witnessed various #InfoSec personalities on Twitter state how much they loathe meetings; sometimes going into great detail.  They will use Slack, email, and phone calls to ensure pointless meetings are non-existent.  To them, meetings on their calendars are present for a legitimate reason; however, this is not necessarily the case for SE.
    Depending on the industry, deliverables are determined by meetings and documents reviewed rather than producing the system itself.  Programs often have Preliminary Design Reviews (PDRs), Critical Design Reviews (CDRs), and Test Readiness Reviews (TRRs) to determine program direction and effectiveness.  These are understandably long meetings which require input and participation from all parties.  Then there are smaller meetings leading up to the big meetings.  Most are tag-ups to gauge progress or work on requirements/specifications matrices.  In reality the vast majority of small meetings are unnecessary busy work designed to get "face-time" with customers and project members.  They are based on archaic principles left over from the corporate world to which older employees are accustomed.  To that end, SE is not a traditionally flexible engineering discipline and lacks forward-looking innovative measures, which is harmful in the long run as it becomes difficult to recruit Millennial and Generation-Z engineers.


  "Butts-in-Seats"

    Although SE contains valid work, some business sectors are inundated with systems engineers to an unnecessary degree.  This plethora causes a depression in the worth of their work.  There are only so many engineers that can be thrown at a requirements document before waste occurs.  It never occurs to managers, stakeholders, or even system owners how much money, time, and effort is wasted at various levels.

    This depression causes tons of downtime and ultimately boredom for more ambitious employees.  As previously mentioned, overcomplication of menial tasks gives an appearance of "productivity;" productivity that is unsolicited.  Appearance is the bottom line.  Over time this becomes exhausting, especially in an office full of inflated egos.  Acronyms are a great example of appearance inflation.  Most of the time they are legitimate and useful for quickly communicating types of systems, sub-systems, parts, and concepts.  In the SE world, acronyms act as a second language and are sometimes made up.  I encountered one systems engineer who regularly created two letter acronyms for his chart packages.  Why would someone do that?  More work is required to track new acronyms and communicate shared understanding to stakeholders.  It ties back to appearance of effort.  Just throw three letters together and think of a name so everyone gets the impression that engineers are conducting "business."  Acronym abuse is rampant and ultimately wasteful.
    Depending on the company, SE is very prone to "body shop" type of work.  This is usually another outcome of depression caused by too many systems engineers.  I have heard from friends and coworkers about jobs in which they were left alone for upwards of three months at a time!  One coworker coined this as "butts-in-seats" type of work where people are hired to fill a contract.  Unsurprisingly these are usually SE positions.


  Analysis

    SE is a discipline that contains incredibly useful principles that should be applied to every engineering field.  At a high level, SE is great for organization and identifying problems and pitfalls in projects.  Remember to use it as a "pinch of salt" to avoid burdening the project.
    Limitations of SE should be considered at the small team level, although a small team will likely never reach them.  Additionally, I consider stand-alone systems engineers as engineering generalists; meaning their work is ultimately overhead for actual engineering teams.  If considering an SE position, the following questions need addressing before the interview:

  1. Are you at least a mid-level engineer?
  2. Do you want to be management?
  3. Do you enjoy meetings?
  4. How long do you wish to stay a systems engineer?
  5. Do you have a plan for graduate school?
  6. Do the Project Management Professional (PMP) and Certified Information Systems Security Professional (CISSP) certifications excite you?
If any responses to these questions are in doubt, SE is likely not for you.  Some technology companies have specialized SE positions that retain technical proficiency, but these are exceptions.  Overall SE principles are fantastic and worth using in projects.  There is a reason systems-based approaches have permeated engineering fields.  On the contrary, I advise against a dedicated SE career as it will likely bore you to tears.  Work instead in specialized areas while incorporating SE's valuable components a la carte.
